What I Can Treat (Urinary Tract Infections)

UTIs are one of the most straightforward infections to diagnose and treat remotely. Here's what I handle:

Acute Cystitis (Bladder Infection)

The most common UTI. Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain. Usually caused by E. coli. Responds quickly to antibiotics.

Uncomplicated UTI

UTI in a non-pregnant, non-immune-compromised person with no anatomical abnormalities. Standard first-line antibiotics work great.

Recurrent UTIs

If you've had multiple UTIs and know the pattern, I can help with acute episodes. We can also discuss prevention strategies.

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

You peed and saw blood, or a lab test showed bacteria but you have no symptoms. Let's figure out if you need treatment or just monitoring.

What I Can't Treat — Go to the ER

  • Symptoms of pyelonephritis (fever, flank pain, severe malaise)
  • Signs of sepsis (fever, chills, confusion, low blood pressure)
  • Pregnancy-related UTI complications
  • Immunocompromised patients with UTI symptoms
  • Retention, inability to void, or complete urinary obstruction
  • Recent urologic surgery or instrumentation with signs of infection
  • Testicular pain or prostatitis symptoms in males

⚠ Emergency Signs: If you have high fever (>102°F), flank/back pain, nausea/vomiting, or feel severely ill — GO TO THE ER. These are signs of kidney infection. This is not a replacement for emergency care.

Important: This is bridge care. I can diagnose and treat the acute infection based on your symptoms. What Should You Do? A Quick Triage Guide

Mild UTI (Annoying But Manageable)

  • Dysuria (burning with urination) — noticeable but tolerable
  • Frequent urination (more than usual but not every 5 minutes)
  • Urge to urinate without much output
  • No fever or very low-grade fever (under 99°F)
  • No flank pain or back pain
  • Feeling otherwise well

  • Intense dysuria (painful to pee, hard to ignore)
  • Very frequent urination (every 30–60 minutes)
  • Suprapubic pain or cramping
  • Low-grade fever (99–101°F) or chills
  • Feeling tired, achy, or generally unwell
  • Urgency so severe it's affecting work or daily life

Severe UTI (Medical Emergency)

  • Fever >102°F or rapid onset fever
  • Flank pain or back pain (sign of kidney involvement)
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Feeling severely ill, confused, or disoriented
  • Chills, sweating, feeling shaky
  • Unable to urinate or urinary retention
  • Signs of sepsis (dizziness, rapid heartbeat, shortness of breath)

Questions? Here Are Answers.

How do you diagnose a UTI without a urine test? +

Classic UTI symptoms — dysuria, frequency, urgency — in a non-pregnant, immunocompetent patient are highly specific for UTI. Studies show symptom-based diagnosis is accurate about 90% of the time. If I'm concerned (recurrent infections, atypical presentation, or pregnancy), I'll ask you to get a urine test to confirm. But if you're a 35-year-old with classic symptoms and no risk factors, we can treat confidently based on what you describe.

How much does it cost? +

$59 flat fee. That's the whole visit — no upsells, no surprise charges, no "extra" fees for the prescription or follow-up questions.

What antibiotic do you prescribe? +

Usually nitrofurantoin (Macrobid) — it concentrates in urine, works against the most common UTI bugs (E. coli, Klebsiella), and side effects are minimal. If you're allergic to nitrofurantoin or sulfa drugs, alternatives are trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or cephalexin. I pick based on your med history, allergies, and what works best for you.

How long does treatment take? +

Typical course is 5 days for uncomplicated UTI. Some recommendations say 3 days, but 5 gives better bacteriologic cure rates. You'll usually feel better within 24–48 hours — pain goes away, urgency improves. Finish the full course even if you feel better.

Do you need a urine sample? +

For uncomplicated UTI in typical patients, no. For recurrent infections, pregnancy, or if I'm unsure, yes — I'll ask you to get a urine culture at a lab or urgent care so we know exactly what we're treating. Most of the time though, symptoms are enough.

What if I'm pregnant? +

UTIs in pregnancy need different antibiotics (nitrofurantoin is okay, but we need a urine test to confirm). Asymptomatic bacteriuria in pregnancy needs treatment. If you're pregnant or think you might be, mention it immediately — we need to handle this differently for you and your baby's safety.

What if it doesn't get better? +

Text me. If symptoms aren't improving after 48 hours, or they're getting worse, we need to reassess. Might be a resistant bug and you need a different antibiotic. Might be pyelonephritis and you need the ER. But don't just sit there suffering — follow up.

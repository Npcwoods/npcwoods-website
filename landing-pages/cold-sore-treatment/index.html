Clinical overview

What a cold sore is, in plain language

A cold sore — also called a fever blister — is a small cluster of blisters on or around the lip, caused by the herpes simplex virus, usually HSV-1 (oral herpes, ICD-10 B00.1). It's extremely common: most adults carry the virus, typically from childhood contact. Once you have it, the virus stays quiet in the body and flares up now and then — often triggered by stress, sun exposure, illness, fever, or being run down. It is not a reflection of anything you did wrong, and it's very treatable.

Why timing matters

Catch it at the tingle

A cold sore moves through predictable stages. Antiviral treatment works best when it's started at the very first sign — the tingle, before a blister forms. Start early and you can shorten the outbreak, and sometimes stop a blister from fully forming at all.

Even if you're already past the tingle, it's still worth texting — treatment can help at later stages too, and we can talk about preventing the next one.

Is it actually a cold sore?

Cold sore vs. canker sore vs. cracked corners

Mouth and lip problems get mixed up all the time. Here's how to tell them apart.

What it is Hallmark signs What it needs
Cold sore (oral herpes) Starts with a tingle, then fluid-filled blisters on the outside of the lip; tends to recur in the same spot ✓ We treat this by text
Canker sore A flat white/yellow ulcer inside the mouth; not contagious; no blisters Usually self-care — text us if it's severe
Angular cheilitis Cracked, sore corners of the mouth; no blisters on the lip itself Sometimes — text us to find out
Spreading or infected rash Honey-colored crust, fast spread, fever, or a sore near the eye Needs in-person eval

Not sure which one fits? Text us — Chris will ask the right questions to point you to the right care.

How we treat it

Two ways to get ahead of it

Cold sores are treated with prescription antiviral medication. Which approach fits depends on how often you get them — text Chris and he'll walk you through it.

For an active flare

Episodic treatment

Start at the first tingle

A short course of prescription antiviral, taken as soon as you feel an outbreak starting. The earlier it begins, the more it can shorten the episode.

If you get them often

Suppressive treatment

Daily prevention

If outbreaks are frequent, a daily preventive option can reduce how often they happen — useful before a big event, a vacation, or a sunny trip.

Comfort & healing

Soothe the spot

Alongside treatment

Keep the area clean, avoid picking, use lip balm with SPF, and a cool compress can ease soreness while the antiviral does its work.

Protect other people

Avoid spreading it

While it's active

Cold sores spread by direct contact — skip kissing and sharing cups, utensils, or lip products until the spot has fully healed, and wash your hands.

Safety first

When to be seen in person

I'm a nurse practitioner, not a salesman. The following situations mean you need more than a text visit.

⚠ Get hands-on care if any of these apply:

  • A sore is near or in your eye, or your eye is painful, red, or light-sensitive — this can threaten vision and needs urgent care
  • An infant or newborn has a cold sore
  • You have a weakened immune system (chemo, transplant, HIV, certain medications)
  • You have eczema and the rash is spreading across the skin
  • A sore hasn't healed within two weeks, or keeps getting bigger
  • Spreading redness, swelling, pus, or fever — signs of a bacterial infection

For the classic stuff — a tingle and a blister on the lip — that's bread-and-butter telehealth.

FAQ

Cold sores — common questions

Can I get cold sore treatment online?

Yes. Text (480) 639-4722 and describe what you're feeling. A double board-certified Nurse Practitioner will review your symptoms and, when appropriate, send a prescription antiviral to your pharmacy. Most patients hear back the same day, usually within a few hours.

How fast should I start cold sore treatment?

As early as possible. Antiviral treatment works best when it's started at the very first sign — the tingle, itch, or burn before a blister forms, ideally within 48 hours. Starting early can shorten the outbreak and sometimes stop a blister from fully forming.

Do I need to send a photo of my cold sore?

Usually not. Cold sores have a classic pattern Chris can recognize from your description. If a photo would help him be sure, he'll ask — but many visits are handled from your text alone.

How much does it cost?

$59 flat. No hidden fees, no surprise bills, no paperwork. Most generic antiviral medications typically run $4–$20 at the pharmacy with GoodRx.

Can you help if I get cold sores often?

Yes. If you get frequent outbreaks, there's a daily suppressive option that can reduce how often they happen. Text Chris about your pattern and he'll talk through whether episodic or suppressive treatment fits you better.

Is a cold sore the same as herpes?

Cold sores are caused by the herpes simplex virus, usually HSV-1. It's extremely common — most adults carry it, typically from childhood contact like a relative's kiss. It is not a reflection of anything you did wrong, and it's very treatable.

When should I be seen in person instead?

Get in-person care if a sore is near or in your eye, if you have a weakened immune system, if it's an infant or newborn, if you have eczema and the rash is spreading, or if a sore hasn't healed in two weeks. Chris will tell you straight up if your situation needs hands-on care.

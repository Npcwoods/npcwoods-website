Clinical overview

What a UTI is, in plain language

A urinary tract infection is a bacterial infection of the urinary system. The most common form — and the one we treat by text — is acute uncomplicated cystitis (bladder infection, ICD-10 N39.0). Most are caused by E. coli from the gut traveling up the urethra. About 50–60% of women will have at least one UTI in their lifetime.

Differential diagnosis

UTI vs. yeast vs. STI vs. kidney

"Down-there" symptoms can be confusing. Here's how to tell what's what.

Condition Hallmark symptoms What it needs
UTI (bladder infection) Burning when you pee, urgency, frequency, suprapubic pressure ✓ We treat this by text
Yeast infection Itching, thick white discharge, redness — no burning while urinating ✓ We treat this by text
STI (chlamydia, gonorrhea) Burning + unusual discharge, pelvic pain, recent new partner Sometimes — text us to find out
Kidney infection UTI symptoms plus fever, chills, back/side pain, nausea Needs in-person eval (ER)

Not sure which one fits? Text us — Chris will ask the right questions to point you to the right care.

Safety first

When to go to the ER instead

I'm a nurse practitioner, not a salesman. If something more serious is going on, I want you in an ER, not texting me.

⚠ Head to the ER if any of these apply:

  • Fever over 101°F with back or side pain — that's kidney territory, may need IV antibiotics
  • Significant blood in your urine (not just a little pink)
  • You can't keep fluids down — can't take oral antibiotics if you're vomiting
  • You're pregnant or think you might be
  • Child under 2 with urinary symptoms

For the classic stuff — burning, urgency, frequency, lower belly pressure — that's bread-and-butter telehealth.

Recurrent infections

Getting UTIs more than once?

A UTI is considered recurrent when you have two or more in six months, or three or more in a year. About 1 in 4 women who get one UTI will have another within six months.

For the acute flare-up — the "it's back again, I know this feeling" episode — a text visit works the same way it does for a first UTI. Chris picks an antibiotic based on what's worked for you before and what hasn't.

What we can't do via text is the longer workup recurrent UTIs sometimes need: urine cultures, post-menopausal estrogen evaluation, imaging, or urology referral. If that's where you are, we'll be straight with you. See the AUA recurrent UTI guideline for what that workup looks like.

FAQ

Common questions

Can I really get UTI antibiotics without going in?

Yes. A licensed Nurse Practitioner at NPCWoods reviews symptoms by text and prescribes same-day antibiotics. This is HIPAA-compliant and fully supported by IDSA clinical guidelines for uncomplicated bladder infections.

What if it's not a UTI?

Chris will ask detailed follow-up questions to identify your condition. We only recommend treatment when your symptom pattern securely fits a UTI. If your symptoms suggest something else, we'll refer you to in-person care and refund your fee immediately.

How much do the antibiotics cost?

Generic UTI antibiotics typically cost under $10 at retail pharmacies with GoodRx. You pay a separate $59 flat fee for the telehealth consult. Prescriptions sent electronically to CVS, Walgreens, Costco, or Walmart.

How fast will I hear back?

Most patients receive a treatment plan within a few hours. Chris reviews all consults same-day, including weekends. Once the review is complete, your Rx is sent electronically to your pharmacy immediately.

How is this different from urgent care?

NPCWoods is $59 flat and entirely over text — no appointments, no waiting rooms, no germ exposure. Walk-in urgent care typically charges over $150. You get direct care from a double board-certified NP without leaving your home.

What if I don't feel better in 48 hours?

Text us immediately if symptoms don't start improving within 48 hours. Follow-up reviews are completely free. We may switch your prescription or recommend you go in for a urine culture.

Can men get UTI treatment online?

Men generally require in-person evaluations since male UTIs are classified as complicated. Text us anyway — Chris will evaluate your symptoms safely and refer you to local care with a full refund if needed.

What if I'm pregnant or might be?

Pregnant patients experiencing urinary symptoms must be evaluated in person by an OB or clinic. UTIs during pregnancy require close monitoring that text-based care cannot safely provide. We do not treat pregnant patients.
